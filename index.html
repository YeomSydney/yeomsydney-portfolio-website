I Wasn't There

Overview

Absence speaks louder than words

<I Wasn't There> is a set of two booklets enclosed in a box cover, exploring the themes of absence and existence through two different poems. It examines how memory, imagination, and personal histories are shaped in the absence of direct experience and how these gaps inform our understanding of existence.

Year

December, 2024

Duration

5 Weeks

Type

    Book

Project Background

Clothing often represents our character almost like an extension of ourselves. What clothing or fabric makes you?

Today, the fashion industry is growing bigger and faster than ever. People spend tremendous time figuring out what suits them and how clothing can present their identity. These days, what we wear easily represents our character. It almost becomes an extension of ourselves.

While realizing this, a question emerged: What clothing makes us? Looking deeper, it turned into: What fabric makes us? From these questions, the exhibition “The Fabric” was created. It also try to listen to people’s thoughts and gather different answers shaped by different experiences.

Challenges & Goals

  • Challenges

    The exhibition needed to be both creative and accessible while staying true to its theme. A key challenge was integrating fashion into exhibition goods such as brochures, tickets, badges, and stickers while working with a minimal colour palette. This required rethinking everyday items and transforming them into meaningful materials that supported the concept.

  • Goals

    The main goal was to design exhibition goods that were both functional and fashion-related, serving visitors and staff alike. Another aim was to create a short brochure that not only guided people through the galleries but also engaged them, encouraging deeper connections with fabrics and the stories presented.

Overall Visual Direction

The design centers on spatial rhythm and typographic presence. A minimal approach allows type and white space to define structure, creating clarity by leaving ample white space. Monochrome photography reinforces the conceptual theme of 'existence and absence'. The lack of colours strips images down to light and shadow, aligning with the theme.

Colour

The colour palette was deliberately limited to blue, black, and white. Blue, serving as the brand’s accent colour, was applied sparingly, while black and white formed the foundation of the visual system. Since the fabrics are the primary focus, the restrained palette ensured they remained the center of attention. White was prioritized for most applications, followed by black, with blue strategically introduced to highlight and enhance key elements without distraction.

Typeface

A geographic sans serif set in uppercase was chosen to evoke precision, control, and a contemporary tone, reflecting the tension between presence and restraint.

The Fabric

Overview

Wear the Unseen

“The Fabric” explores how deeply clothing shapes who we are. Fashion is more than style. It reflects identity, character, and emotion. In this exhibition, we use the word “fabric” not only for shirts, pants, and garments, but as a metaphor for the unseen threads that weave ourself. Each piece asks "what fabric makes you?"

While realizing this, a question emerged: What clothing makes us? Looking deeper, it turned into: What fabric makes us? From these questions, the exhibition “The Fabric” was created. It also try to listen to people’s thoughts and gather different answers shaped by different experiences.

Discover/Define

How can exhibition materials extend visitor experiences?

Through research and market analysis focusing on exhibitions, it became clear that visitors enjoy to physically interact with what they see. For example, they might touch, feel, or hear the works, or leave writings on a sticky note and attaching it to a wall.

Since this exhibition centers on artworks made of fabric and on speaker presentations, it was important to think about materials that could meaningfully connect the exhibition, fashion, and the visitors. From this, the idea emerged to reinterpret everyday fashion—related items, such as hang tags, receipts, packaging, and tape, and blend them with typical exhibition materials such as tickets, stickers, and guides, etc.

Discover/Define

Which materials can integrate with fashion-inspired items?

Because the artworks themselves could not be altered, the focus turned toward exhibition-related materials. Items such as admission tickets, gallery guides, brochures, and fabric guides were redesigned to incorporate elements of fashion-related materials, including hang tags, labels, paper bags, and receipts.

The integration was organized into groups as following:

  • Tickets + Hang Tags
    Hang tags, typically used to present clothing details such as size or colour, were reinterpreted as tickets. This pairing emphasizes their shared role of providing essential information, linking the fashion context to the exhibition entry.
  • Gallery Guide + A Receipt
    Receipts, an everyday commercial format, share common ground with guides in that both communicate information to consumers. By reimagining the gallery guide as a receipt, the design can highlight consumers' experiences in a familiar and accessible way.
  • Fabric Guide + Fabric Labels
    Fabric labels, traditionally used to communicate material information, were adapted as the medium for the fabric guide. This direct connection can reinforce the material qualities of the exhibition.
  • Brochure (A Standalone Piece)
    It was left as a standalone piece to ensure clear and accessible presentation of information.

Outcome

Overall Visual Direction

The visual direction prioritizes restraint. Exhibition materials are designed to recede, keeping the focus on the fabric artworks while maintaining a refined, contemporary tone.

Colour

The colour system is limited to blue, black, and white. White and black form the structural base, while blue functions as an accent, introduced selectively to emphasize key elements. The restrained palette ensures the fabrics remain the visual focus.

Typeface

A slightly loose sans serif typeface in thin weights was selected to introduce subtle character without overpowering the system. The light typographic treatment reinforces the exhibition’s minimal and refined tone.

Brass Fork

Overview

Snatch Your Burger!

Brass Fork is a casual burger house serving fresh, flavourful burgers, vegetarian options, hand-picked sides, and sweets, made to feel both familiar and a little special. From signature dipping sauces to comforting classics, every dish is crafted with balance, care, and flavour. The brand embraces simplicity and friendliness, creating comfort around a well-made burger.

Project Background

Clothing often represents our character almost like an extension of ourselves. What clothing or fabric makes you?

Brass Fork aims to present both its signature burgers and vegetarian options in a welcoming, approachable way. As a casual burger house, it offers an accessible space where people can relax and enjoy their meals with ease. To meet diverse needs, Brass Fork has created special vegetarian burgers and seasonal menu items that showcase their enthusiasm and care for food.

Challenges & Goals

  • Challenges

    To match its friendly and accessible character, Brass Fork wanted a mascot that embodies a casual, doodle-like vibe. This mascot would feature across a refreshed, colourful (yet not overwhelming) paper menu, as well as packaging designs. The illustrations serve as the most significant element of the brand, balancing accessibility and friendliness with a sense of seriousness and quality.

  • Goals

    The priority was to create a mascot and complementary doodle illustrations that reflect Brass Fork’s personality. These illustrations guided the packaging design, keeping it simple, casual, and fun. At the same time, colour and typography choices were carefully considered to ensure harmony across all brand elements.

Discover/Define

What do brand characters represent?

By researching brand characters used by other fast–food franchises, it became clear that these mascots often represent the brand’s values, personality, and inclusivity. For Brass Fork, the character concept was developed to avoid defining features such as hair, gender, or age. This neutrality allows the character to represent a broad audience, reflecting Brass Fork’s openness to people of all genders, ages, and dietary preferences. A simplified design also makes the character easier to express through doodles, keeping it approachable and flexible.

Discover/Define

What makes a brand look and feel friendly?

Friendliness in branding often comes from warm, welcoming gestures and the use of positive, attentive language. For Brass Fork, this friendliness can extend into confident yet caring communication, showing customers that their presence and opinions truly matter. Smiles—both literal and visual—play a crucial role in shaping consumer perception, directly reinforcing the brand’s image. Responsiveness is another key element: quick, thoughtful replies to questions or feedback demonstrate that the brand listens and is willing to adapt.

From this analysis, the character for Brass Fork could embody confidence, enthusiasm, and a slightly bold personality (in a positive, energetic way). By interacting with the fork as a central object—perhaps even using it playfully, like a cannon—the character can symbolize readiness, willingness, and a spirit of action.

Discover/Define

What doodle-style illustration can represent Brass Fork?

Brass Fork is positioned as an accessible and friendly casual burger house. To communicate this, doodles can be illustrated with clean, confident lines—showing that the brand is approachable yet professional, not overly playful. Since Brass Fork was founded with the aim of building closer connections between people and food, the doodles can embody this through posture and interaction.

A fork itself becomes the most symbolic element, representing the brand directly. When paired with a person or character, it conveys a human-first, friendly spirit. Color can be used selectively, either to fill shapes or highlight areas, rather than outlining, ensuring the doodles remain simple and bold.

Solution

Overall Visual Direction

The overall visual direction emphasizes an approachable brand identity through its character, vibrant colours, and a minimal design system supported by solid layouts and grids. The use of structured grids gives the brand strength and a sense of weight. Navy, the darkest and heaviest colour, is applied as the dominant tone, reinforcing seriousness and stability. A serif font further strengthens this impression. To balance this, red and yellow are carefully integrated to bring lightness and friendliness. As a result, the brand achieves a dual quality: heavy and serious, yet approachable and casual.

Colour

The primary palette—blue, red, and yellow—was chosen for its simplicity, which communicates effectively with the illustration style. To maintain clarity and avoid clutter, the brand applies a limited palette guided by a 6:2:1 ratio: navy (60%), yellow (20%), and red (10%). Navy communicates calmness and reliability, yellow conveys joy and enthusiasm, and red adds warmth and appetite appeal. Together, they create a balanced and intentional system.

Typeface

While the character illustration expresses friendliness, the serif font introduces reliability and calmness, balancing the overall brand identity. It also conveys weight and authority, reflecting the brand’s experience and credibility.

Lights on the Water, Where We Become One.

Lights of Seoul is a lantern festival held on Toronto Island that reinterprets Korean cultural traditions within a Canadian context. Through radiant lantern artworks, the festival transforms light into a shared cultural experience—inviting visitors into a collective space of celebration, reflection, and connection.

Project Background

Transferring cultural festival from Seoul, Korea to Toronto, Canada

Lights of Seoul reimagines the Seoul Lantern Festival (서울빛초롱축제) as a site-specific cultural event in Toronto. The project explores how a traditional Korean festival can be translated into a multicultural environment while preserving its original spirit.

Rooted in the theme of gathering, wishing, and unity, the festival adopts the message “Lights on the water, where we become one.” Set in Toronto—a city shaped by diverse cultural backgrounds—the festival reframes light as a symbol of connection, bringing people together through shared experience rather than cultural familiarity alone.

The project involved designing a complete festival system, from core identity to applied materials, ensuring cohesion across physical and digital touchpoints.

Challenges & Goals

‘Letterwaves’ visualizes the frequency of each letter in English through the metaphor of sound. Vertical waves represent how often each letter appears. Based on data from the 1990s to today, it reveals how we write, speak, and shape language.

Overview

The Hidden Transformations of Fairy Tales

Behind the Story is a collection of three classic fairy tales; "Cinderella", "Sleeping Beauty", and "Snow White". Over time, these tales have been retold in countless versions by different authors and creators. Their genres have shifted dramatically from horror to comedy and beyond. This book brings together these diverse interpretations of the chosen stories, ultimately tracing the hidden transformations of each tale and revealing the layers behind the versions we think we know.
