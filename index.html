Gentle Dazs

Overview

Wear your flavour

Gentle Dazs is a conceptual collaboration between Gentle Monster and Häagen-Dazs, reimagining how a product can engage multiple senses through design. Rooted in the idea of “Wear Your Flavour,” the project explores how flavour can be translated into a visual and physical experience.

Project Background

I wore (Vanilla) today. What are you wearing?

This project explores how two distinct brands – one rooted in fashion and the other in food — can collaborate while preserving their individual identities.

Gentle Monster leads the visual and conceptual direction, while Häagen-Dazs contributes its recognizable flavour profiles and emotional associations. Together, they form a shared narrative where flavour becomes something that can be seen, not just tasted. Inspired by Häagen-Dazs’ philosophy of “Make Every Day Extraordinary,” the project reframes everyday moments into something expressive, tactile, and memorable.

Challenges & Goals

  • Challenges

    The central challenge was translating the abstract idea of “flavour” into a physical, visual form that could exist beyond food. This required designing sunglasses that visually express flavour without being literal, creating packaging that functions as both container and experience, and balancing two distinct brand identities while maintaining coherence. The solution needed to feel playful yet refined, conceptual yet tangible.

  • Goals

    The goal was to create a multi-sensory experience that connects product, packaging, and emotion. Rather than focusing solely on aesthetics, the project aimed to transform the act of opening a package into an experiential moment, reinforce brand identity through form, colour, and material, and leave a lasting impression that extends beyond the product itself.

Project Background

Transferring cultural festival from Seoul, Korea to Toronto, Canada

Lights of Seoul reimagines the Seoul Lantern Festival (서울빛초롱축제) as a site-specific cultural event in Toronto. The project explores how a traditional Korean festival can be translated into a multicultural environment while preserving its original spirit.

The Fabric

Overview

Wear the Unseen

“The Fabric” explores how deeply clothing shapes who we are. Fashion is more than style. It reflects identity, character, and emotion. In this exhibition, we use the word “fabric” not only for shirts, pants, and garments, but as a metaphor for the unseen threads that weave ourself. Each piece asks "what fabric makes you?"

Year

Project Background

Clothing often represents our character almost like an extension of ourselves. What clothing or fabric makes you?

Today, the fashion industry is growing bigger and faster than ever. People spend tremendous time figuring out what suits them and how clothing can present their identity. These days, what we wear easily represents our character. It almost becomes an extension of ourselves.

While realizing this, a question emerged: What clothing makes us? Looking deeper, it turned into: What fabric makes us? From these questions, the exhibition “The Fabric” was created. It also try to listen to people’s thoughts and gather different answers shaped by different experiences.

Challenges & Goals

  • Challenges

    The exhibition needed to be both creative and accessible while staying true to its theme. A key challenge was integrating fashion into exhibition goods such as brochures, tickets, badges, and stickers while working with a minimal colour palette. This required rethinking everyday items and transforming them into meaningful materials that supported the concept.

  • Goals

    The main goal was to design exhibition goods that were both functional and fashion-related, serving visitors and staff alike. Another aim was to create a short brochure that not only guided people through the galleries but also engaged them, encouraging deeper connections with fabrics and the stories presented.

Discover/Define

How can exhibition materials extend visitor experiences?

Through research and market analysis focusing on exhibitions, it became clear that visitors enjoy to physically interact with what they see. For example, they might touch, feel, or hear the works, or leave writings on a sticky note and attaching it to a wall.

Since this exhibition centers on artworks made of fabric and on speaker presentations, it was important to think about materials that could meaningfully connect the exhibition, fashion, and the visitors. From this, the idea emerged to reinterpret everyday fashion—related items, such as hang tags, receipts, packaging, and tape, and blend them with typical exhibition materials such as tickets, stickers, and guides, etc.

Discover/Define

Which materials can integrate with fashion-inspired items?

Because the artworks themselves could not be altered, the focus turned toward exhibition-related materials. Items such as admission tickets, gallery guides, brochures, and fabric guides were redesigned to incorporate elements of fashion-related materials, including hang tags, labels, paper bags, and receipts.

The integration was organized into groups as following:

  • Tickets + Hang Tags
    Hang tags, typically used to present clothing details such as size or colour, were reinterpreted as tickets. This pairing emphasizes their shared role of providing essential information, linking the fashion context to the exhibition entry.
  • Gallery Guide + A Receipt
    Receipts, an everyday commercial format, share common ground with guides in that both communicate information to consumers. By reimagining the gallery guide as a receipt, the design can highlight consumers' experiences in a familiar and accessible way.
  • Fabric Guide + Fabric Labels
    Fabric labels, traditionally used to communicate material information, were adapted as the medium for the fabric guide. This direct connection can reinforce the material qualities of the exhibition.
  • Brochure (A Standalone Piece)
    It was left as a standalone piece to ensure clear and accessible presentation of information.

Outcome

Overall Visual Direction

The visual direction prioritizes restraint. Exhibition materials are designed to recede, keeping the focus on the fabric artworks while maintaining a refined, contemporary tone.

Colour

The colour system is limited to blue, black, and white. White and black form the structural base, while blue functions as an accent, introduced selectively to emphasize key elements. The restrained palette ensures the fabrics remain the visual focus.

Typeface

A slightly loose sans serif typeface in thin weights was selected to introduce subtle character without overpowering the system. The light typographic treatment reinforces the exhibition’s minimal and refined tone.

Visual System
